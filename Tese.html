Using embeddings to infer diagnostics from medical prescriptions

A major challenge in public health is the timely detection of diseases, as early diagnosis improves outcomes and helps control the spread of communicable diseases. However, traditional diagnostic and forecasting tools are slow and expensive, often relying on surveys and reports. Fast monitoring of both communicable and non-communicable diseases requires alternative approaches.

This project explored the use of electronic prescription records (EPRs) for estimating disease prevalence through Natural Language Processing (NLP) methods. The central hypothesis is that patterns within prescription data could help infer diagnoses, allowing for accurate real-time disease monitoring.

Using the Portuguese EPRs database, from 2017 to 2021, we first generated embeddings for active substances and clustered them to identify groups of substances prescribed within the same context. Using this method we not only retrieved meaningful lists of substances associated with predefined medical conditions, we could even identify substances mistakenly omitted from an expert-curated validation dataset.

By associating the clusters found with known medical conditions, we inferred potential diagnoses without relying on explicit diagnostic data, addressing a significant gap in EPRs. In particular, our estimates for diabetes prevalence aligned with published values, obtaining an estimated diabetes prevalence of 5.1% in Portugal for 2019.

The study found that NLP applied to EPRs can estimate non-communicable disease prevalence and may be used for near real-time disease monitoring. These may provide a scalable, cost-effective tool for public health surveillance.




The Master's thesis was developed in the 2nd semester of 2023/2024. The project was co-supervised by LIP researchers Lília Perfeito and Joana Gonçalves de Sá, both from the SPAC Lab (https://socialcomplexity.eu) at LIP, and by Bruno Martins (http://web.ist.utl.pt/bruno.g.martins/) from IST and INESC-ID.
